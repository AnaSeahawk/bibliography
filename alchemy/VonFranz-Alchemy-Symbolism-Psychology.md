## Key quotes

### The wingless bird as prima materia (p. 127)

> The wingless bird is the red sulphur; it is the bird below, and is also referred to as the female, so that we have a paradox because, being driven, it is regarded as the male active quality but projected onto the lower bird it is the female. So the characteristics female-male are very vague; the terms are used in different ways in alchemy. It could be said that the wingless bird, the red sulphur, is an underlying factor of the inner psychic life and is always what one has first to unearth, for it is the *prima materia*.

And on meeting it (p. 128):
> When you have met them, you are at the bottom, and then you are in the *prima materia* and there you touch it. You meditate on it and do active imagination, or you look for the underlying meaning.

---

### The despised stone as goal (p. 169)

> It is a strange thing, if we look at it naively, that in alchemy the end product is something which in the order of nature we look upon as very low, namely a stone, something whose quality is just to be there. A stone neither eats nor drinks nor sleeps, it just remains there for all eternity. If you kick it, then it stays where you kicked it and does not move. But in alchemy this despised thing is the symbol of the goal.

---

### Trodden in the streets — Sophia as prima materia (p. 187)

Quoting the Arabic alchemical text on the Wisdom of God:
> Come, my sons, and listen, for I will teach you the Wisdom of God, who is wise and who understands that about which Alphidius says that adult people and children pass it in the street, that it is stamped into the dung every day by the animals there, and of which Senior says nothing is outwardly more despised and nothing in nature more precious and God has not given it to be bought with money.

---

### The feminine Sophia calling from the streets (p. 189)

> At first one is rather puzzled because there is a paraphrase of the words of the Wisdom of God. She appears in the streets, she calls for men... And then the thought shifts, and it says: "This is the thing which is trodden upon in the streets, it is despised by everybody."

Von Franz's gloss on the same passage:
> Nothing is outwardly more despised and nothing in nature more precious...to find her only one thing is needed, namely a subtle perception of the true nature.

---

### Alchemy dug from the dunghill (p. 13)

Von Franz on Jung's discovery of alchemy:
> Dr. Jung has devoted many years of study to this subject, which he practically dug up from the dunghill of the past, for it was a forgotten and despised field of investigation which he has suddenly revived.

## Archive relevance

- **Women of Alchemy essay**: the wingless bird/female as prima materia quote is a direct citation for the argument that alchemy coded female body as base material. The trodden-in-streets Sophia passage grounds the Cleopatra/Mary the Jewess lineage — wisdom as the thing underfoot.
- **Translation map**: the despised stone passage. The goal is what looks lowest — counter to every heroic framework.
- **Manifesto**: prima materia as "what must first be unearthed" before any transformation — parallels the distillation baseline argument.
- **Inanna essay**: Sophia called from the streets / trodden upon = Inanna stripped at the gates. Same archetypal structure, different cultural register.
