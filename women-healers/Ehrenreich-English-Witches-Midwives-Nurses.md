## Key quotes

### The witch as empiricist (part0006_split_003)

> The witch-healer's methods were as great a threat to the Catholic Church, if not the Protestant, as her results, for the witch was an empiricist: she relied on her senses rather than on faith or doctrine, she believed in trial and error, cause and effect. Her attitude was not religiously passive, but actively inquiring. She trusted her ability to find ways to deal with disease, pregnancy, and childbirth—whether through medications or charms. In short, her magic was the science of her time.

---

### The triple threat (part0006_split_003)

> She was a triple threat to the Church: She was a woman, and not ashamed of it. She appeared to be part of an organized underground of peasant women. She was a healer whose practice was based in empirical study. In the face of the repressive fatalism of Christianity, she held out the hope of change in this world.

---

### Witch-healers as the only medicine available (part0006_split_003)

> Witch-healers were often the only general medical practitioners for a people who had no doctors and no hospitals and who were bitterly afflicted with poverty and disease. In particular, the association of the witch and the midwife was strong: "No one does more harm to the Catholic Church than midwives," wrote witch hunters Kramer and Sprenger.

---

### The Church's offer (part0006_split_003)

Michelet, *Satanism and Witchcraft*, quoted:
> On Sundays, after Mass, the sick came in scores, crying for help, and words were all they got: "You have sinned, and God is afflicting you. Thank him; you will suffer so much less torment in the life to come. Endure, suffer, die. Has not the Church its prayers for the dead?"

---

### Organized campaign — not hysteria (part0006_split_001)

> The witch craze was neither a lynching party nor a mass suicide by hysterical women. Rather, it followed well-ordered, legalistic procedures. The witch hunts were well-organized campaigns, initiated, financed, and executed by Church and State.

---

### Real scope (part0006_split_001)

> One writer has estimated the number of executions on an average of 600 a year for certain German cities—or two a day, "leaving out Sundays." Nine hundred witches were destroyed in a single year in the Wertzberg area... At Toulouse, four hundred were put to death in a day. In the Bishopric of Trier, in 1585, two villages were left with only one female inhabitant each. Women made up some 85 percent of those executed—old women, young women, and children.

---

### Anti-empiricism as doctrine (part0006_split_003)

> The Church, by contrast, was deeply anti-empirical. It discredited the value of the material world, and had a profound distrust of the senses. There was no point in looking for natural laws that govern physical phenomena, for the world is created anew by God in every instant.

Quoting *The Malleus* on Augustine:
> This evil, which is of the devil, creeps in by all the sensual approaches; he places himself in figures, he adapts himself to colors, he attaches himself to sounds, he lurks in angry and wrongful conversation, he abides in smells, he impregnates with flavours and fills with certain exhalations all the channels of the understanding.

## Archive relevance

- **Women of Alchemy essay**: direct historical evidence for the suppression argument. The "triple threat" quote is the single best summary of why women's healing knowledge was targeted — not because it failed, but because it succeeded outside patriarchal structures.
- **Sovereign biophysics**: the empiricist passage supports the epistemological claim — knowledge grounded in sensory experience and trial-and-error is in this lineage. The witch as scientist precursor.
- **Amaroli / urine-therapy archive**: indirectly relevant — the suppression of empirical home medicine is the same structure that marginalized body-based practices.
- **Manifesto**: the Church's offer ("endure, suffer, die") is the exact opposite pole to the archive's epistemology.
