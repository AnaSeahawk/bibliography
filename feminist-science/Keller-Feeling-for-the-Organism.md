## Contents and key arguments

This biography of Barbara McClintock — Nobel laureate, discoverer of genetic transposition — is foundational for the archive's epistemology. Keller uses McClintock's method to argue that science grounded in attentive relationship with a subject constitutes legitimate knowledge.

**Central arguments to locate:**

- McClintock's description of her method: "I was part of the system... you need to look and look and ask the material itself to tell you what it is doing."
- Her practice of "listening to the corn" — direct attentive presence as methodology
- Keller's framing: "a feeling for the organism" as an epistemological stance, not a soft intuition but an active, rigorous form of knowing
- The corn genetics career: rejected for decades precisely because her method was relational, not reductionist
- Her isolation from the scientific community — the parallel to the witch-healer suppression argument
- The Nobel recognition decades late: the institution eventually confirmed what she already knew

## Extracted quotes

### The program is dynamic — and takes instructions from the whole (Preface)

> With the discovery of genetic mobility, our very concept of a genetic program has changed. It needs now to be thought of as a dynamic structure, rather than as a static linear message inscribed in the sequence of DNA. And to the extent that the movement of genes is itself part of the program, we need to ask: Where do the instructions come from? McClintock's answer—that they come from the entire cell, the organism, perhaps even from the environment—is profoundly disturbing to orthodox genetics.

### Knowledge and the tangled web (Preface)

> But the actual story is both more complex and more illuminating. It is a story about the nature of scientific knowledge, and of the tangled web of individual and group dynamics that define its growth.

## Archive relevance

- **Sovereign biophysics**: the single-subject methodology defense. McClintock's career is the closest scientific parallel to the archive's epistemological stance — one person, over years, in detailed relationship with a system. Her work was valid before it was recognized.
- **Women of Alchemy essay**: McClintock is the modern heir of Mary the Jewess — a woman whose method was relational rather than extractive, and who was sidelined by institutional science for it.
- **Feminist science section**: pairs with Haraway (*Simians, Cyborgs, and Women*) and Harding (*The Science Question in Feminism*) as grounding for "situated knowledge."
- **Manifesto**: the direct epistemological citation — "knowledge that lives in relationship with its subject" is what the archive does. Keller named this before it had a name.
