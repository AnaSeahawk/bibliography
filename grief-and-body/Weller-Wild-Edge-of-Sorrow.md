## Key quotes

### Grief and aliveness (Ch. 1 — *An Apprenticeship with Sorrow*)

> There is some strange intimacy between grief and aliveness, some sacred exchange between what seems unbearable and what is most exquisitely alive. Through this, I have come to have a lasting faith in grief.

> I see this work as soul activism, a form of deep resistance to the disconnected way in which our society has conditioned us to live. Grief is subversive, undermining our society's quiet agreement that we will behave and be in control of our emotions. It is an act of protest that declares our refusal to live numb and small.

---

### *Gravis* — the weight of grief (Ch. 2 — *To and from the Soul's Hall*)

> The territory of grief is heavy. Even the word carries weight. Grief comes from the Latin word *gravis*, meaning "heavy," from which we also get grave, gravity, and gravid. We use the word *gravitas* to speak of a quality in some people who are able to carry the weight of the world with a dignified bearing. And so it is, when we learn to carry our grief with dignity.

---

### Grief hardening to stone (Ch. 2)

> When grief remains unexpressed, however, it hardens, becomes as solid as a stone. We, in turn, become rigid and stop moving in rhythm with the soul. When we are in touch with all of our emotions, on the other hand, we are more verb than noun, more a movement than a thing. But when our grief stagnates, we become fixed in place, unable to move and dance with the flow of life.

---

### Descent in an ascent culture (Ch. 2)

> It is challenging to honor the descent in a culture that primarily values the ascent. We like things rising—stock markets, the GDP, profit margins. We get anxious when things go down... When all we are shown is the imagery of ascent, we are left to interpret the times of descent as pathological; we feel that we are somehow failing.

Robert Bly, quoted by Weller:
> "How can we get a look at the cinders side of things when the society is determined to create a world of shopping malls and entertainment complexes in which we are made to believe that there is no death, disfigurement, illness, insanity, lethargy, or misery? Disneyland means 'no ashes.' "

---

### The well of grief (Ch. 2)

> What do we find there in the well of grief? Darkness, moistness that turns our eyes wet and our faces into streams of tears. We find the bodies of forgotten ancestors, abandoned dreams, ancient remnants of trees and animals—things that have come before and that have the power to lead us to the place to which each of us will return one day when we, too, leave this life, which has been gifted to us for a short time. This descent is a passage into what we are, creatures of earth.

---

### Semipermeable membranes (Preface)

> We are not isolated cells partitioned off from other cells; we have semipermeable membranes that make possible an ongoing exchange with the great body of life. We register in our psyches, consciously or not, the fact of our shared sorrows. Learning to welcome, hold, and metabolize these sorrows is the work of a lifetime.

---

### Ritual and communal grief (Ch. 2)

> The participants' bodies are giddy with joy—a wild alchemy of sorrow and joy, played out once again, as it always has been, in the container of sacred ritual.
